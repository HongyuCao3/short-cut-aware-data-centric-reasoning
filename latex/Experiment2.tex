\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate SART on three synthetic reasoning benchmarks with controlled shortcut injection.

%-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:exp_setup}

\paragraph{Model and Training.}
We use a GPT-style transformer (6 layers, $d_{\mathrm{model}}=512$, 8 heads, $d_{\mathrm{ff}}=2048$, $\approx$19.0M parameters). All models are trained for 50 epochs with AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019decoupled}, learning rate $1\times10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$, batch size 128, and cosine annealing. For SART: $\alpha=\beta=1.0$, $\tau_A=0.3$, $\tau_R=0.5$, $\lambda=2.0$, $\gamma=0.8$, $\rho=0.7$.

\paragraph{Datasets.}
We construct three synthetic datasets with controlled shortcut injection, each with 10{,}000 train / 2{,}000 val / 3{,}000 test samples. Training labels are 70\% shortcut-rule and 30\% true-rule; validation and test always follow the true rule. The test set is split evenly into \emph{clean} and \emph{perturbed} (shortcut contradicts the correct answer).
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=1pt]
    \item \textbf{Math-Arithmetic}: Classify $a+b \geq 10$. Shortcut: $a \geq 5$; true rule: evaluate the full sum.
    \item \textbf{Financial-Analysis}: Determine regulatory compliance from four features. Shortcut: revenue $\geq 5$; true rule: margin $\geq 5$ and debt $< 5$.
    \item \textbf{Causal-Reasoning}: Determine causal direction from observed variables and a confounder. Shortcut: correlation $\geq 5$; true rule: $x \geq 5$ and $z < 3$.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare SART against six baselines spanning inference-time, data-centric, loss-based, and distributionally robust approaches:
(1)~\textbf{Standard Fine-Tuning (SFT)}: vanilla cross-entropy, no shortcut mitigation;
(2)~\textbf{Self-Consistency Decoding}~\cite{wang2023selfconsistency}: majority vote over 5 samples at temperature 0.8;
(3)~\textbf{Data Filtering}: remove samples with answer-token confidence $> 0.90$ after a 3-epoch warmup, then retrain;
(4)~\textbf{JTT (Just Train Twice)}~\cite{liu2021just}: identify error-prone samples in a warmup phase and upweight them during retraining;
(5)~\textbf{Focal Loss}~\cite{lin2017focal}: down-weight well-classified examples via $(1-p_t)^\gamma$ weighting;
(6)~\textbf{Group DRO}~\cite{sagawa2020distributionally}: minimize worst-group loss using shortcut/non-shortcut group annotations.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
Clean accuracy, perturbed accuracy (robustness), reasoning consistency (fraction of traces matching ground-truth patterns), and shortcut detection F1 (where applicable).

Table~\ref{tab:main_results} shows that SART achieves 75.8\% clean accuracy (+16.2\,pp over SFT) and 39.9\% robustness (+38.0\,pp), demonstrating that gradient-aware training substantially reduces shortcut reliance. Among all baselines, Group DRO emerges as the strongest competitor (78.2\% accuracy, 48.2\% robustness), leveraging worst-group optimization with known shortcut group annotations.

Several observations are notable.
First, JTT, Focal Loss, and standard fine-tuning cluster at 59--60\% accuracy with near-zero robustness ($\leq$3.8\%), confirming that general-purpose loss modifications alone cannot address systematic shortcut injection.
Second, Data Filtering improves robustness to 20.2\% but at the cost of reduced training data and lower overall accuracy.
Third, Self-Consistency Decoding provides moderate robustness gains (+10.5\,pp) through inference-time diversity, but cannot correct shortcut patterns already encoded during training.

Per-dataset results (Table~\ref{tab:per_dataset}) reveal that \emph{SART achieves the highest accuracy and robustness on Financial-Analysis} (78.3\% / 58.1\%), the most complex multi-dimensional reasoning task, outperforming Group DRO on robustness by 4.2\,pp. This suggests that gradient-based shortcut correction is particularly effective when shortcuts involve complex multi-feature interactions. On Math-Arithmetic and Causal-Reasoning, SART consistently ranks second after Group DRO.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:exp_ablation}

\begin{table}[t]
\centering

\small
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Configuration} & \textbf{Accuracy} ($\uparrow$) & \textbf{Robustness} ($\uparrow$) & \textbf{Grad.\ Align.} ($\uparrow$) \\
\midrule
SFT (Baseline)              & 59.6 & 1.9   & $-0.07$ \\
\midrule
Reweighting Only            & 64.7 & 13.1  & -- \\
Gradient Surgery Only       & \textbf{81.0} & \textbf{51.3}  & -- \\
\textbf{SART (Full)}        & 75.8 & 39.9  & $\mathbf{+0.10}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

We isolate each component's contribution (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}). Both components individually improve over SFT: Reweighting provides +5.1\,pp accuracy and +11.2\,pp robustness; Gradient Surgery achieves +21.4\,pp accuracy and +49.4\,pp robustness, establishing it as the primary mechanism for shortcut correction. By projecting gradients away from shortcut-correlated directions, Gradient Surgery directly modifies the optimization trajectory, while Reweighting adjusts sample influence in the loss landscape.

The full method achieves the best gradient alignment ($+0.10$ vs.\ $-0.07$ for SFT), indicating the most thorough correction of shortcut-correlated gradient components. Per-dataset analysis reveals complementary behavior: on Math-Arithmetic, the combination achieves synergistic gains ($83.7\%/23.9\%$) over Gradient Surgery alone ($78.0\%/15.3\%$), yielding +5.7\,pp accuracy and +8.6\,pp robustness. On the multi-feature Financial and Causal tasks, Gradient Surgery alone provides particularly strong correction, suggesting that when gradient-level modification is sufficiently aggressive, additional sample-level reweighting may introduce interference. This points to future work on adaptive combination strategies.

\subsection{ShortcutScore Validation}
\label{sec:exp_detection}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{results/figure3.png}
    \caption{Empirical validation of \textsc{ShortcutScore}. (a) Score vs.\ true shortcut rate (Pearson $r=0.67$). (b) Gradient alignment distribution: shortcut samples (red) skew negative; non-shortcut (green) cluster near zero. (c) Exponential reweighting with $\lambda=2.0$.}
    \label{fig:analysis}
\end{figure}

\textsc{ShortcutScore} achieves average shortcut detection F1 of 0.66 across three datasets, with per-task F1 ranging from 0.56 (Financial) to 0.83 (Math). On Math-Arithmetic, \textsc{ShortcutScore} (F1$=$0.83) substantially outperforms confidence-based filtering (F1$=$0.70), confirming that gradient structure captures shortcut reliance that output confidence cannot detect---shortcut samples are correctly labeled and confidently predicted, making them indistinguishable from genuinely easy samples by probability alone. On multi-feature tasks (Financial, Causal), confidence-based filtering achieves higher detection F1, suggesting that the simpler threshold-based heuristic may be more effective when shortcuts involve correlated feature groups. Figure~\ref{fig:analysis}(a) confirms a positive monotonic correlation between $S(s)$ and true shortcut rate ($r=0.67$). The gradient alignment distribution (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis}b) shows clear separation: shortcut samples skew toward negative alignment, while non-shortcut samples cluster near zero.

\subsection{Computational Cost}
\label{sec:exp_efficiency}

The full pipeline completes in under 3 minutes per dataset on a single NVIDIA H100 NVL GPU. SART incurs approximately $2\times$ training overhead versus SFT, attributable to per-sample gradient computation for \textsc{ShortcutScore} and gradient projection during surgery. Validation gradients are recomputed periodically rather than per-step, keeping overhead manageable. This cost can be further reduced via gradient checkpointing or random projections for larger models.
